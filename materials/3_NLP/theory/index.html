Computational Linguistics


  • Computational Linguistics: interdisciplinary field concerned with the processing of language by computers (Mitkov, 2004); develops computational methods and formalisms to answer linguistic questions
    • Natural Language Processing: solves engineering problems arising from the analysis and generation of natural language text (written or spoken)

Example NLP tasks and applications

  • Information Extraction: extracting names, dates, places, etc.
  • Machine Translation: translating languages
  • Coreference resolution: "Elena told Greta she needed to rest"
  • Word Sense Disambiguation: "I have to buy a mouse"
  • Sentiment Analysis: automatically determine affective states in text
  • Topic Modelling: discovering topics in one or more texts
  • ... and more!

Why is NLP difficult?

  • Language ambiguity, e.g. homography, word sense
  • Idiomatic expressions, e.g. "bee's knees"
  • Neologisms, e.g. "Brangelina"
  • Non standard language, e.g. "4eva" or "YEEESSSS"
  • Tokenisation/segmentation, e.g. "new York apartment" vs. "New York apartment"
  • Lack of annotated data (especially with regard to ancient languages) for algorithm training

How do we solve these problems?

  • Language resources and data
  • Rule-based methods
    • Rational approach (intuition based): human experts define rules
    • Advantage: Accuracy can be very high
    • Disadvantages:
      • Language-dependent
      • Building and maintaining rules is expensive
  • Machine Learning
    • Empirical, probabilistic approach (data-driven): machine autonomously learns the rules
    • Advantages:
      • Language-independent
      • More automation and improvement over time
    • Disadvantages:
      • Requires lots of annotated data
      • Less transparent
  • Hybrid methods: rule-based + Machine Learning

Syntactic parsing

Syntactic parses make use of syntactically annotated data or  treebanks . This level of annotation typically includes:

  • Lemmatisation (disambiguated)
  • Morphological traits (disambiguated)
  • Syntax annotation
    • Content words are connected to one another through dependency relations
    • Function words depend on the content word they modify

Semantic parsing

Process of assigning meaning, a sense, to the correct syntactic structure = disambiguation

Semantic parsing

Begins with an analysis of relations between words.
This requires knowledge of:

  • Semiotic connotation and collocation, that is, words that occur frequently with other words, e.g. idioms, metaphors or similes such as "green with envy"
  • Lexical hierarchies and classes, WordNet, VerbNet, FrameNet, etc.).

Semantic parsing

Examples:

  • Machine Translation
  • Semantic role labeling: process of assigning labels to words or sentences denoting their semantic role in a wider context, e.g. agent, goal or result
  • Word Sense Disambiguation: assigning semantic labels to identify and disambuate the sense of a word in a given sentence
  • Sentiment Analysis: identifying and classifying sentiment and affective states in text. Used also to predict outcomes. Makes use of:
    • sentiment lexica: words are classified according to positive/negative/neutral polarity, strength of sentiment, particular types of attitude, etc.
    • SentiWordNet: WordNet annotated for degrees of positivity/negativity/neutrality
